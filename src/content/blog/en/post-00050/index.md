## Part 1

Ok, I owe you a good update.

I will do the interview with Sophia, or Gemma, as soon as possible, but first there are a lot of things I need to tell you.

We left off quite some time ago (it was December 14th) and we were left with the good news that we could rent the house where we are now.

We moved to Alphen aan den Rijn (pronounced "AL-fun ahn den RINE").

Moving this time was quite nerve-wracking.

The delivery of the keys to the new house was planned for February 16th and we were supposed to leave the house in Leiden on February 28th. As I have already told you, here in Holland they have the nice habit of renting houses without floors. So we would have had to do all the floors (except the bathroom and the downstairs toilet), remove the wallpaper from the walls and paint them, buy and install all the appliances (the new kitchen was only equipped with a cooking plate), put lights in all the rooms, carpet the two internal stairs, and other minor works.

Furthermore, we would have had to box up all our belongings, transport them to the new house, and deliver the Leiden house intact, so as not to risk part of the security deposit.

On Monday, February 16th, I went to pick up the keys to the house alone, Hilly and the girls were in Tuscany and would only return on Friday.

Upon entering the new house I was immediately confronted by mixed feelings. On the one hand, I was happy with the new home and the new chapter in our adventure, but on the other hand, I found myself facing a mountain of work to do in very little time! Help!!!!

I took two days off from work to start with the floor. In my naive optimism, I thought I would finish all the floors by Friday, in the two days off plus the evenings after work, but the harsh reality was instead that the delivery of the laminate was delayed and I could only start the floors on Saturday, a week before the move.

Long story short, only thanks to the work of Hilly, Sophia, Gemma, and their respective boyfriends, we succeeded in the titanic enterprise.

On Saturday, February 28th, moving day, there were 7 of us, we worked all day without stopping, and after four trips by van (rented) and car, almost everything had been transported.

At sunset, in agreement with our 3 helpers who were observing Ramadan, we rewarded ourselves with an excellent dinner from a Yemeni takeaway and ate on a blanket on the concrete floor, among a thousand boxes, in our new house. On Sunday Hilly and I returned to the Leiden house to pack the last things and sort everything out; the delivery of the keys had fortunately been postponed to Monday morning.

To date I have finished 90% of the floors and the house is starting to get cozy. The garden looks like a building materials dump but soon we will restore it to its splendor.

We really like the house and the neighborhood is also very welcoming and full of greenery.

Alphen aan den Rijn, however, alas, has nothing to do with Leiden. It's a bit of a commuter city, or whatever you want to call it.

The center isn't bad, full of shops, and it stretches along the Rhine River. It also has a long history, given that it was founded by the Romans, under the name of "Castellum Albanianae," and at the time, 2000 years ago, it was an important commercial center. However, outside the historic center, the outskirts, especially near where we live now, are quite horrible.

Large public housing buildings and wide streets heavily trafficked by numerous cars alternate with pleasant clusters of small dutch houses and parks. We are in the cluster of small dutch houses.

All four of us miss Leiden, with its thousand canals and small streets constantly infested with bicycles. The very few cars, the young and creative frenzy of students in perpetual activity.

Our heart remained there, and maybe later we'll return, but for now we are here in Alphen, and slowly we will get used to it.

The girls still go to school in Leiden and Hilly also still works there.

We left two of our many bicycles locked at Leiden central station; when the girls need them they can use them, or when we go there for other reasons.

At the moment, every morning, we all take the train at Alphen aan den Rijn station: the girls go to Leiden, I go to The Hague, and Hilly, even if at different times, also goes to Leiden.

Our house is 5 km from the station; in the morning I cycle there, Sophia too. Gemma, instead, refuses to do it. She says that on the train everyone is packed together and there is a bad smell, and she gets nauseous. So she takes the bus, which takes 50 minutes to get to Leiden.

Hilly has even cycled to work from home already. A round trip is 40 km and she went twice at 5 in the morning and once she returned after the evening shift, cycling from 11 to midnight. But there was a beautiful moon illuminating the Dutch countryside.

I'm doing well in the new job, I go every day to The Hague except on Wednesday, the only day I go to another hotel in the chain, the Qbic hotel, in Amsterdam, where they don't have a maintenance worker; they are desperately looking for one but can't find him. The hotel manager has already asked me twice if I know anyone looking for a position like that. The work is very varied and not at all boring, I get along well with Laurens, born and raised in The Hague 25 years ago. It's just him and me looking after the maintenance of the two hotels, while I go to Amsterdam alone.

In the last month we have been busy building a mini workshop and our own small warehouse for spare parts stock inside the Ald hotel, where we are constantly looking for spaces to occupy with our tools or stocks.

Since both hotels are in the historic center of The Hague, the spaces are very narrow and it takes creativity to make the most of them.

## Part 2

I wrote part one of this post a few weeks ago but I wasn't able to post it. As I said before, I haven't made life easy for myself with the technical part of the blog since I wrote the code myself; every time I want to post a new message I have to waste quite a bit of time, and then I give up. Soon I want to restructure all the code, also using AI, in order to make it easier to make new posts.

In recent weeks spring has exploded and Alphen aan den Rijn is blooming like fields of tulips and magnolias. In front of our house there are always children playing or people walking their dogs. Undoubtedly I'm starting to like this place.

We all already feel at home and the wound in our hearts that opened when we left Leiden is healing.

The house is very cozy and we can finally breathe a sigh of relief and relax a bit during the weekend.

Two Sundays ago we went to visit "Archeon", a theme park dedicated to local history and prehistory. It's located right here in Alphen and we cycled there. It's very well done, there are reconstructions of dwellings and structures from the Neolithic to the Middle Ages, passing through the Romans. All structures are made of wood, stone, concrete and brick and you can go inside to see the interiors. There are many actors/artisans who do the work that was done in the past and take everything very seriously. A really recommended place, for children but also for adults.

Spring in Holland is something really special. Partly because you start to see the sun and it's finally pleasant to be outdoors, after the long rainy winter, but also because there are flowers everywhere. Not to mention the families of ducks and swans that roam the city as if nothing were happening.

In the center of The Hague, where I go almost every day, the streets are populated with tourists or locals sitting at bar tables, music and scents all around, scents of food and grass, the good kind.

The Hague, as I already told you, I like a lot. Compared to Amsterdam it is much less chaotic but there are so many things to do and see.

I'll see you soon with the interview with Sophia. She will have many things to tell, she will change school next year, to come to Alphen, but then the year after that, to go to University, in The Hague or in Rotterdam, she hasn't decided yet.
